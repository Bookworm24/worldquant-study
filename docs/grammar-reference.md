# English Grammar Reference

## Overview

This document provides a comprehensive reference for English grammar rules, designed to support your learning journey. Each section includes explanations, examples, and practice notes.

## 1. Parts of Speech

### Nouns
- **Definition**: Words that represent people, places, things, or ideas
- **Examples**: dog, city, happiness, teacher
- **Types**: 
  - Common nouns: boy, book, car
  - Proper nouns: John, London, Microsoft
  - Abstract nouns: love, freedom, knowledge
  - Concrete nouns: table, water, bird

### Pronouns
- **Definition**: Words that replace nouns
- **Examples**: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
- **Types**:
  - Personal: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
  - Possessive: my, your, his, her, its, our, their
  - Demonstrative: this, that, these, those
  - Relative: who, which, that

### Verbs
- **Definition**: Words that express actions, states, or occurrences
- **Examples**: run, be, have, think, become
- **Types**:
  - Action verbs: run, eat, write
  - Linking verbs: be, seem, appear
  - Helping verbs: can, will, have, be

### Adjectives
- **Definition**: Words that describe or modify nouns
- **Examples**: beautiful, large, red, happy
- **Function**: Provide more information about nouns

### Adverbs
- **Definition**: Words that modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs
- **Examples**: quickly, very, extremely, yesterday
- **Function**: Describe how, when, where, or to what extent

### Prepositions
- **Definition**: Words that show relationships between nouns and other words
- **Examples**: in, on, at, by, with, from
- **Function**: Indicate location, time, direction, or other relationships

### Conjunctions
- **Definition**: Words that connect words, phrases, or clauses
- **Examples**: and, but, or, because, although
- **Types**:
  - Coordinating: and, but, or, so
  - Subordinating: because, although, when

### Interjections
- **Definition**: Words that express sudden emotions
- **Examples**: Oh! Wow! Hey! Ouch!

## 2. Sentence Structure

### Basic Sentence Patterns
- **Subject + Verb (SV)**: Birds fly.
- **Subject + Verb + Object (SVO)**: She reads books.
- **Subject + Verb + Complement (SVC)**: He is happy.
- **Subject + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object (SVOO)**: I gave him a book.
- **Subject + Verb + Object + Complement (SVOC)**: We elected him president.

### Sentence Types
- **Declarative**: Makes a statement (e.g., "I like coffee.")
- **Interrogative**: Asks a question (e.g., "Do you like coffee?")
- **Imperative**: Gives a command (e.g., "Please close the door.")
- **Exclamatory**: Shows strong emotion (e.g., "What a beautiful day!")

### Sentence Structures
- **Simple**: Contains one independent clause (e.g., "I went to the store.")
- **Compound**: Contains two or more independent clauses (e.g., "I went to the store, and I bought some milk.")
- **Complex**: Contains one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses (e.g., "I went to the store because I needed milk.")
- **Compound-complex**: Contains multiple independent and dependent clauses

## 3. Verb Tenses

### Present Tenses
- **Simple Present**: I work, He works
- **Present Continuous**: I am working
- **Present Perfect**: I have worked
- **Present Perfect Continuous**: I have been working

### Past Tenses
- **Simple Past**: I worked
- **Past Continuous**: I was working
- **Past Perfect**: I had worked
- **Past Perfect Continuous**: I had been working

### Future Tenses
- **Simple Future**: I will work
- **Future Continuous**: I will be working
- **Future Perfect**: I will have worked
- **Future Perfect Continuous**: I will have been working

## 4. Active vs. Passive Voice

### Active Voice
- Subject performs the action
- Example: "The chef cooked the meal."

### Passive Voice
- Subject receives the action
- Example: "The meal was cooked by the chef."
- Formation: be + past participle

## 5. Conditionals

### Zero Conditional
- For general truths
- Structure: If + present simple, present simple
- Example: "If you heat water, it boils."

### First Conditional
- For real possibilities in the future
- Structure: If + present simple, will + base verb
- Example: "If it rains, I will stay home."

### Second Conditional
- For hypothetical or unlikely situations
- Structure: If + past simple, would + base verb
- Example: "If I won the lottery, I would travel the world."

### Third Conditional
- For past hypothetical situations
- Structure: If + past perfect, would have + past participle
- Example: "If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam."

## 6. Modal Verbs

### Ability
- **can**: I can swim.
- **could**: I could swim when I was younger.
- **be able to**: I am able to swim.

### Permission
- **can**: Can I leave early?
- **may**: May I leave early?
- **could**: Could I leave early?

### Obligation
- **must**: You must complete the assignment.
- **have to**: I have to wake up early.
- **should**: You should eat more vegetables.

### Possibility/Probability
- **may**: It may rain tomorrow.
- **might**: It might rain tomorrow.
- **could**: It could rain tomorrow.

## 7. Articles

### Definite Article: "the"
- Used for specific or previously mentioned nouns
- Example: "The book I read yesterday was interesting."

### Indefinite Articles: "a/an"
- Used for non-specific nouns
- Example: "I saw a dog in the park."
- Use "an" before vowel sounds: "an apple"

## 8. Common Grammar Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Subject-Verb Agreement
- Correct: "The list of items is long."
- Incorrect: "The list of items are long."

### 2. Misplaced Modifiers
- Correct: "I saw the man with the telescope."
- Better: "I saw the man who had a telescope."

### 3. Double Negatives
- Correct: "I don't have any money."
- Incorrect: "I don't have no money."

### 4. Confusing Similar Words
- "Your" vs. "You're": Your = possessive; You're = you are
- "Its" vs. "It's": Its = possessive; It's = it is
- "There" vs. "Their" vs. "They're": There = location; Their = possessive; They're = they are

## 9. Punctuation

### Period (.)
- Ends declarative sentences
- Example: "She went to the store."

### Comma (,)
- Separates items in a list
- Sets off introductory elements
- Example: "After the meeting, we went to lunch."

### Semicolon (;)
- Joins related independent clauses
- Example: "I have a big test tomorrow; I can't go out tonight."

### Colon (:)
- Introduces lists or explanations
- Example: "I need three things: paper, pens, and books."

## 10. Common Phrases and Constructions

### Gerunds vs. Infinitives
- **Gerund**: Verb + -ing (functioning as noun)
- Example: "Swimming is fun."
- **Infinitive**: to + base verb
- Example: "I want to swim."

### Relative Clauses
- **Defining**: "The book that I borrowed is interesting."
- **Non-defining**: "My brother, who lives in London, is visiting."

## Learning Tips

1. **Practice Regularly**: Grammar rules become natural with consistent practice
2. **Read Widely**: Exposure to correct grammar in context
3. **Write Regularly**: Apply grammar rules in your own writing
4. **Ask Questions**: When uncertain, seek clarification
5. **Use Examples**: Connect rules to concrete examples
6. **Review Regularly**: Revisit rules periodically to maintain knowledge

Remember: Grammar is a tool for clear communication. Focus on understanding the rules but don't let perfect grammar prevent you from expressing yourself.
